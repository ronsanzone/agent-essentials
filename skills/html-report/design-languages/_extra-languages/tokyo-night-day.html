Design Language

Tokyo Night Day, read in daylight

The light editorial inversion — proving Tokyo Night's blue-and-purple logic isn't tied to a dark canvas. Newsreader serif headings on cool paper, Geist body, deep blue authority.

01

Personality

Day is the daylight register of Tokyo Night. The palette inverts — the indigo backgrounds become cool paper grey (#e1e2e7), the lavender foreground becomes a deep blue ink (#3760bf) — but the relationships hold: blue is still the primary accent, purple is still secondary, orange is still warn. Two reports across Storm and Day should look like the same publication printed for night-shift and day-shift readers.

The serif/sans split echoes Editorial Parchment but the register is cooler. Newsreader is a reading-optimized serif with strong italic forms — it carries display headings and ledes with enough authority that the document feels like a serious editorial document rather than a tech-blog template. Geist sans handles body. JetBrains Mono carries code, eyebrows, marginalia, and pills. There is no paper grain — the background is cool paper, not parchment; warmth would betray Tokyo Night's identity.

Day proves Tokyo Night isn't a dark theme. It's a color logic that survives the inversion.

The blue here is #2e7de9 — deeper and more saturated than Storm's #7aa2f7 because it has to do its work on a light background. The blue-deep variant (#1a5dc8) carries hover states. Purple (#9854f1) is more saturated than its Storm equivalent for the same reason. Orange (#b15c00) is genuinely dark — Day's warn is a serious admonition, not a glowing terminal warning.

02

When to pick this

Pick this when

Reports going to mixed audiences (engineers + PMs + executives). Documents that may be exported to PDF, printed, or projected in well-lit rooms. Long-form technical writing where editorial register matters. Reports that need a daylight register but want to stay in the Tokyo Night family rather than switching to Editorial Parchment.

Don't pick this when

The report is heavily code-led (terminal output, shell sessions, CLI tooling) — Moon's mono register suits that material better. The audience self-identifies as engineer-internal and prefers dark documents — use Storm. Documents under ~5 minutes — Day's serif chrome feels overdressed at short length.

Optical-size settings

  • h1.title: font-variation-settings: "opsz" 72 — Newsreader's largest optical size, most refined letterforms for display.
  • h2: opsz 48.
  • h3: opsz 30.
  • Ledes and summaries: opsz 24–28.
  • Pull quotes: opsz 32.
  • Captions: opsz 20 — the smallest optical size used.

Voice rules

  • Italic Newsreader for ledes, pull quotes, captions, and italic-em phrases in titles: <h1>Title, <em>italic phrase</em></h1>.
  • Mono caps with letter-spacing for eyebrows and meta-row keys.
  • Never bold body prose for emphasis — use italic instead. Bold is reserved for callout labels and h4.
  • Pull quotes are large (≥24px), italic serif, set off by a thin left rule.
  • The eyebrow dot is always var(--purple) — the language's smallest purple touch and the cousin of Storm/Moon's accent dot.
  • Generous whitespace. Day's prose breathes more than Editorial Parchment's — it reads as a daylight-newspaper register rather than a long-form-essay register.

05

Anti-patterns

  • No warm backgrounds. Cream, parchment, or any tint warmer than #e1e2e7 breaks the Tokyo Night cool-paper identity. If the document wants warmth, use Editorial Parchment instead.
  • No pure white (#fff) backgrounds. The base is the exact #e1e2e7 grey-blue.
  • No Inter, Roboto, or Arial. Day's body is Geist; display is Newsreader.
  • No system-font defaults. The variable axes and italic forms in Newsreader are load-bearing — fallback serifs lose them.
  • No heavy drop shadows. Cards lift with 0 6px 20px rgba(46, 60, 95, 0.05). Heavier shadows kill the daylight feel.
  • No light blue (#7aa2f7 from Storm) — it lacks contrast on Day's light background. Use the deeper #2e7de9.
  • No emoji in headings or callouts.
  • No bold + italic combined. If italic is in use, bold is off (and vice versa).

07

Diagram philosophy

Diagrams in Day are quiet and precise — thin rule borders, generous padding, bg-card fill, blue arrows. Text in diagrams uses Geist (matching body) so diagrams stay in conversation with the prose. The deeper blue (#2e7de9) carries arrows and hot nodes; purple (#9854f1) carries external nodes.

Alert region pattern

When a diagram has a failure path or exceptional region, wrap the affected messages in an orange-tinted dashed rectangle and place an inline alert pill (orange-soft fill + orange border + orange-colored mono caps text). The pattern is visible in the sequence diagram above between messages 03 and 04.

LockedWarn-tinted background uses rgba(177, 92, 0, 0.06) + dashed orange border. The inline alert pill stacks a colored dot, a mono-caps label, and a Newsreader italic detail.
FreeRegion size, exact alert pill copy, whether to include the colored dot.

Arrow color

Default arrows: var(--blue). 2px stroke for primary flow, 1.5px for secondary references. Return paths in a sequence diagram use arrow-muted at var(--rule-strong) (#8990ab) so direction is legible at a glance.

Caption

Italic Newsreader at opsz 20, var(--ink-faint), 14px, centered under the diagram with margin-top: 12px.
