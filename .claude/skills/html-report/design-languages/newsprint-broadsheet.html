Design Language

Newsprint Broadsheet, set in column rules

A typographic register for annual reports, long-form journalism, financial disclosures, and any document that wants the authority of a printed newspaper.

01

Personality

Newsprint Broadsheet is the contemporary broadsheet register — a Sunday paper read at the kitchen table, or the back-page essay in The New York Times Magazine. The document feels like it was typeset by someone who has done this for a hundred years. Authority is conveyed through restraint: one accent color, generous heavy display type, thin column rules, and a body face built for endurance reading.

A newspaper is a document that has earned the right to use one red flag and nothing else.

The Playfair/Georgia split is load-bearing. Headlines are Playfair Display at heavy weights — a high-contrast didone-style serif that gives the page its broadsheet bone structure. Body is Georgia, the canonical web newspaper serif, because nothing carries 1,500 words of investigative prose like Georgia. Section labels, datelines, and meta keys lean on Oswald, the condensed sans that newspapers reach for when they want a label to feel like it was set in lead. Code and column numbering use JetBrains Mono.

02

When to pick this

Pick this when

Annual reports and yearly retrospectives. Long-form journalism mockups. Financial disclosures, S-1 narratives, decade-in-review pieces. Documents that want the authority of a printed newspaper, where the reader will sit and absorb sequentially.

Don't pick this when

The content is short or operational (use a lighter register). The content needs warmth and conversation (try editorial-parchment). The content is dense classified data — use the newsprint-classified sibling. The audience expects modernity over tradition.

Voice rules

  • Headlines are Playfair Display at 800–900. Tight letter-spacing (-0.02em to -0.025em). The didone-style high-contrast strokes are the brand.
  • One red flag and nothing else. The accent is --red-flag-nyt #C8102E — used on the eyebrow flag, italic emphasis in h1, the progress bar, pills, and section numbers. Do not introduce a second hue.
  • Mono caps with letter-spacing for Oswald labels (eyebrow, byline, h4, table headers).
  • Pull quotes are large, italic Playfair, framed top/bottom with thin black rules, opened with a red curly quote.
  • Never bold body Georgia for emphasis — use italic.
  • Body is HIGH CONTRAST #111111 on cream. Ink on newsprint.

Diagram-kit aliases

Newsprint is monochromatic plus one flag color. The diagram-kit's secondary slot maps to ink — not a second hue.

--accent:         var(--red-flag-nyt);
--accent-soft:    #fadfe4;
--secondary:      var(--ink-primary);
--secondary-soft: #e2dccb;
Warning: Do not introduce a second saturated accent. Newsprint authority comes from one red flag against high-contrast ink. Add blue or green and the document stops feeling like a newspaper.
05

Anti-patterns

  • No Inter, Roboto, system-ui defaults.
  • No dark themes — broadsheet is light-only.
  • No box-shadows. Newspapers don't cast shadows. Borders are thin black rules, not soft shadows.
  • No second saturated accent. One red flag and nothing else.
  • No rounded card corners over 0px on cards — broadsheets don't have rounded corners. Diagram boxes inside SVGs may have small radii (12–14px) but card chrome is square.
  • No emoji in headings, no gradient buttons.
  • No sans-serif body. Georgia or nothing.
  • No light-weight display type. Playfair below 700 weight on headings reads as the wrong language.

07

Diagram philosophy

Broadsheet diagrams are quiet newspaper infographics — the kind drawn in ink by a staff illustrator and printed at 85 dpi on rag paper. Fills are not paint; they are hatchings — diagonal rules, cross-hatch, vertical lines — defined as inline SVG <pattern>s inside each diagram's <defs>. The only color in any diagram is a single red flag (#C8102E) on the hot role; everything else is black ink. Labels sit outside the boxes, connected by thin guide-lines, and captions begin with an Oswald-caps FIG. 1 — prefix.

SVG typography

Body copy in diagrams is Georgia; everything that names a thing — actor labels, step numbers, alert banners — is Oswald caps. There is no monospace register in these diagrams; the typewriter voice belongs to a different language.

.msg       { font: 13px Georgia, serif; fill: var(--ink-primary); }
.note      { font: italic 11px Georgia, serif; fill: var(--ink-faint); }

Alert region pattern

The alert region is a dense cross-hatched rectangle (#seq-hatch-warn — 4px-spaced perpendicular diagonals) with a 1px black border. Banner copy sits on paper-white inserts cut out of the hatch so the Oswald caps stay legible. The pattern is visible in Fig. 2 — between steps 3 and 4.

LockedDense cross-hatch ground; black border; paper-white banner inserts for labels. Alert-arrow accent uses --red-flag-nyt — the same red flag as the hot role.
FreeRegion size, exact banner copy, number of nested banners.

Arrow stroke

Default arrows: black --ink-primary at 1.25px — ink on paper. Return paths use arrow-muted at 1px --ink-faint so direction is legible. Async dispatch is dashed black. The red flag appears only on the alert-region arrow.

Caption convention

Captions begin with an Oswald-caps FIG. N — prefix in --ink-primary, followed by Georgia italic sentence-case description. The em dash after the figure number is the period of the newspaper register — never replace it with a colon.
