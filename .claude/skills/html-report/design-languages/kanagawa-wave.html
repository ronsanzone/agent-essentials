Design Language

Kanagawa Wave, indigo and foam

A dark, artisanal register modeled on Hokusai's woodblock print — sumi-ink backgrounds, fuji-white parchment text, and crystal-blue accents the colour of wave-foam reflecting a moonlit sky.

01

Personality

Kanagawa Wave is a Japanese woodblock print rendered as code. Imagine reading a long technical document by lamplight on washi paper — the page is a deep indigo-night, the text the warm yellowed cream of aged parchment, and the accents are the cold cyan of wave-foam against a stormy sky. The signature is the --fuji-white body text: it is never stark, never bone-white; it is the colour of a paper lantern at dusk.

If the foreground reads as pure white on the screen, the variant has been mis-rendered. Fuji-white is warm, yellowed, and dim by design.

The font stack leans heavily on Noto Serif JP — a Japanese-aware serif that handles Latin script with grace and brings the woodblock register without resorting to caricature. JetBrains Mono provides the technical counterpoint for code and marginalia. The accent triple is intentional: crystal-blue is the wave-foam (primary, restrained), wave-aqua the deeper teal that catches subtitles and the eyebrow dot, and surimi-orange the warn — the colour of a torii gate against the storm.

02

When to pick this

Pick this when

Dense architecture tours read at night. PR reviews that span multiple subsystems and benefit from the slower reading rhythm a serif imposes. Operator references where the reader will sit and absorb 20+ minutes of text and expects technical density paired with visual quiet.

Don't pick this when

Short field reports under five minutes — the artisanal register feels overdressed. Incident postmortems where outcomes-forward bluntness matters more than register. Light-mode environments — for those, reach for kanagawa-lotus.

Voice rules

  • Italic Noto Serif JP for ledes and pull quotes — keep weight at 300 or 400, never bold-italic.
  • Mono caps with letter-spacing: 0.18em for eyebrows; 0.12em for marginalia numbers.
  • Pull quotes use the same crystal-blue as the h1 italic — they continue the title's voice into the body.
  • The eyebrow dot is always --wave-aqua with a soft glow shadow — this is the brand's smallest teal touch.
  • Never bold body prose for emphasis — use italic. Bold weights are reserved for h4 and <strong> inside callouts.

05

Anti-patterns

  • No pure white #FFFFFF for body text. Fuji-white #DCD7BA is the signature — stark white reads as a different brand.
  • No Inter, Roboto, or system sans for body. The Noto Serif JP register is load-bearing.
  • No light backgrounds — that is what kanagawa-lotus exists for.
  • No saturated neon accents. The Wave palette is cool and muted; an electric blue or fluorescent green breaks the woodblock illusion.
  • No drop-shadows heavier than 0 14px 40px rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.25). Heavier shadows kill the flat-paper feel.
  • No emoji or kanji as decoration. The Japanese influence is in the typography, not in pasted-in glyphs.
  • No three-accent combinations. Crystal-blue plus wave-aqua is the duo; surimi-orange is reserved for warn only.

07

Diagram philosophy

Diagrams here are ink-on-rice-paper, not rounded rectangles. Brushstroke arrows curve gently (never straight). A subtle wave-pattern SVG fill textures store and external nodes. Node corners are asymmetric (rx ≠ ry) to suggest a hand-drawn form. Small wave-aqua dots sit near labels like ink drops. Captions are italic Noto Serif JP, continuous with the body prose.

Default brushstroke arrows: crystal-blue at 1.5px, round caps, gentle quadratic curve. Return paths use brush-arrow-muted (sumi-ink-6) so direction reads at a glance. Async/fire-and-forget: same crystal-blue brush with stroke-dasharray: 6 3. Warn: surimi-orange at 1.8px.
